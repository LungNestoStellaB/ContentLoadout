**Script (Clean for Teleprompter - 3m 15s):**
If you want to understand the future of global power, stop looking at the land. Look down. The Clarion-Clipperton Zone stretches across the Pacific Ocean, between Hawaii and Mexico. It is massive. And sitting on the mud at the bottom are potato-sized chunks of metal called polymetallic nodules. They took millions of years to form. They are packed with cobalt, nickel, copper, and manganese. 

Every single electric vehicle on the road, every wind turbine spinning on a hill, and every supercomputer processing artificial intelligence requires these metals. But the mines on land are running dry. The supply chains are choked. So, the biggest economies on Earth have turned their eyes to the abyss.

But you cannot send a human miner down thirteen thousand feet. The pressure would crush a car like an aluminum can. You need machines. Ghost fleets. Right now, autonomous underwater vehicles—AUVs—are gliding over the seafloor. They have no tethers. They are not remote-controlled. They use artificial intelligence to map the terrain, dodging deep-sea canyons and black smoker vents, operating entirely in the pitch black.

They are looking for the richest deposits. And trailing right behind them are the harvesters. Massive, tank-like machines that roll across the seabed, vacuuming up the nodules and pumping them miles up to ships waiting on the surface.

This is not science fiction. It is happening right now. And the stakes could not be higher. We are talking about the raw materials required to own the twenty-first century. 

But there is a catch. The deep ocean is not empty. It is a delicate, ancient ecosystem. Every time a harvester rips across the bottom, it kicks up a cloud of sediment that can suffocate life for miles. Some of these species haven't changed in millions of years, and they are being wiped out before we even know they exist. It is the ultimate dilemma. To save the surface of the planet from climate change, we might have to destroy the bottom of the ocean.

Who makes the rules down there? The International Seabed Authority. A small organization in Jamaica that most people have never heard of. They hold the keys to the largest mining operation in human history. 

We are entering an era where the most critical infrastructure on Earth—the cables that carry our data and the metals that power our technology—are entirely invisible to ninety-nine percent of the population. The ghost robots in the Pacific are drawing the new borders of the world. Whoever controls the seabed controls the future.
